About

The journal of a 17-year-old student, with three aims:

(1) To learn as much as he can about what it means to be human.

(2) To learn as much as he can about himself, and his life, in how he thinks about questions. Think of these entries as little "essays"—originally a "test" (in French) of how we respond to a subject.

(3) To offer something useful and interesting to fellow travellers, eager to explore our existence.

Now more than ever, thinking is becoming a rare thing. If you wonder about things and want to learn, you're welcome here.

On the performance of being

Consider the strange recursion at the heart of authenticity. The moment one becomes conscious of being authentic, one is already performing authenticity—and thus the very object of pursuit recedes. This morning I observed myself editing not my reaction to an event, but the anticipated retelling of that reaction. Does this secondary processing corrupt the primary experience, or reveal that all experience is always already mediated by narrative?

Perhaps there is no pre-narrative self to access. Perhaps the search for authenticity is itself the performance, a recursive loop that generates the very thing it seeks to escape. The authentic self as a horizon that retreats with each step toward it.

5 February 2026

The narrative cage

Watched someone I have known for years behave exactly as I predicted they would. The accurate prediction should have validated my understanding, yet it produced only unease. Why? Because I realized I had long ago stopped seeing them—I was merely consulting my compressed file on them, a narrative summary that substituted for observation.

We trap people in our stories about them. This might be necessary; holding the full complexity of another consciousness in mind may be cognitively impossible. But that doesn't make it less violent. Intimacy as necessary reduction. Understanding as a form of flattening. We mistake our maps for territories and call it knowledge.

31 January 2026

The question as dwelling

Some questions do not want answers. They want to be inhabited, lived with, returned to. I once thought this was intellectual cowardice—that every question deserved resolution. Now I suspect the opposite: that demanding resolution of fundamentally ambiguous questions is where meaning dies.

Human experience may be structurally ambiguous. The question mark not as placeholder awaiting replacement by a period, but as the architecture of the thing itself. Consider: "What is the meaning of life?" versus "How shall I live meaningfully with uncertainty?" The first demands an answer that will end inquiry. The second invites a practice that sustains it.

Contrast with Descartes' method of doubt—he wanted certainty, not dwelling
28 January 2026

Conversational topology

Every dialogue is a negotiation over attention's finite resource. The interruption, the redirect, the "that reminds me"—each a bid in an economy of relevance. What fascinates is how rarely we acknowledge the negotiation. We pretend the conversation follows organic interest while it actually traces the collision of competing claims to conversational space.

This is not malice. It is geometry. Two minds, one linear thread. The friction is structural. Yet we take it personally, as if the redirect were rejection rather than the inevitable cost of sharing attention. Perhaps all communication is controlled collision, and what we call understanding is finding a rhythm in the chaos.
